# Skill 06 — The Hard Message Drafter

> *"You handled it verbally. But now they need to hear it in writing — and you need it to land the same way it did in the room."*

---

## What this is for

The performance conversation you've been putting off. The one where the numbers have been off for six weeks and you keep hoping it turns around before you have to say something. The one where they think things are going fine and you don't.

Most managers handle these verbally. Which is right — these conversations should happen in person or on a call, not in a Slack message. But verbal-only has a problem: the rep walks away with their own version of what was said. You walk away hoping they heard you. They walk away hoping it wasn't that serious.

The follow-up message closes that gap. It's not a formal write-up. It's a short, direct note that confirms what was said, names what needs to change, and makes clear that you're in their corner — without being so soft that the urgency disappears.

This prompt builds that message. It also builds the prep you need before the conversation itself.

---

## The moment this solves

You had the conversation. Or you're about to. Either way, you need something in writing that:

- Reflects what was actually said
- Doesn't sound like a threat but doesn't bury the stakes
- Keeps the relationship intact while being completely honest
- You could hand to HR if it came to that, but doesn't read like you're building a case

---

## The base prompt

```
I need help preparing for and following up on a hard performance 
conversation with one of my reps.

Here is the context:

**Situation type:** Performance — missing number, conversation delayed 
too long.

**Rep context:**
[Describe the rep — tenure, what performance looked like before, 
what you genuinely think is going on. Be honest, not diplomatic.]

**What the data shows:**
[Paste or describe their numbers.]

**What I've already said:**
[Nothing, one vague comment, multiple conversations? Be specific.]

**What I want them to walk away knowing:**
[Not what you want to say — what you want them to understand.]

**My concern about how it lands:**
I want this to land as direct and caring — not an attack.

---

Give me two things:

**1. Pre-conversation prep**
Three bullet points — the specific things I need to name in the 
conversation. Plain language. For me, not them.

**2. The follow-up message**
150-200 words. Slack or email. Should:
- Open by acknowledging the conversation happened
- Name the specific performance gap without softening it away
- State what needs to change and in what timeframe
- End with something genuine that reinforces I'm in their corner

First person. Direct and warm. No corporate language. No "per our 
discussion." If it drifts formal, pull it back.
```

---

## Example output

**Input:** Rep at 40-50% quota for 3 months after a strong start. Activity still decent. Mentioned it twice in 1:1s but never named the severity. Want her to know I haven't given up on her but this is a 30-day situation.

**Pre-conversation prep:**
- Name the trend explicitly: "The last three months have been 40-50% of quota. I haven't said that number out loud to you before. I'm saying it now."
- Separate activity from outcome: "You're still putting in the work. The problem isn't effort — it's conversion. That's actually good news because it's fixable."
- Name the timeline: "I want to give you 30 days with a specific focus area. Not as an ultimatum — as a plan."

**Follow-up message:**

> Hey [Name],
>
> Thanks for being open in our conversation today. I know it wasn't easy to sit in some of that.
>
> I want to put in writing what I said: the last three months have been around 40-50% of quota, and that's a gap we need to close together over the next 30 days. The good news is your activity is still there — this is a conversion problem, not an effort problem, and those are solvable.
>
> Here's what I'm committing to: more in your corner on call reviews this month. I need you to come to our 1:1s ready to dig into what's happening between the connect and the ask.
>
> I'm not writing this to scare you. I'm writing it because you deserve to know exactly where we stand and exactly what I'm going to do to help.
>
> Let's close this gap.

## The before-conversation variant

When you need to set up the conversation in writing first:

```
Same context, but write me a short message I can send before we meet that:
- Signals the upcoming 1:1 is a real conversation, not routine
- Doesn't alarm them or make them spiral before we talk
- Is honest that I want to discuss performance specifically
- Is 3-4 sentences — enough to prepare them, not enough to replace the conversation

Tone: Like a manager they trust, not an HR department.
```

## Make it yours

```
I want to calibrate how I communicate hard messages. Ask me:

1. How would your best rep describe your style when things are going well?
2. How would they describe it when you have to deliver hard news?
3. Is there a gap between those two? What do you want to close?
4. What's one phrase you've used in a hard conversation that actually worked?
5. What's your biggest fear about how a hard message lands?

Then write me a communication style block.
```

## Tips from the field

**Write the message before the conversation, not after.** Even if you don't send it, drafting the follow-up forces you to get clear on what you actually need to say.

**"No corporate language" is the most important instruction.** Formal language creates distance. Distance makes people defensive.

**150-200 words is the ceiling for a reason.** Long messages let you hide. When you're specific and short, there's nowhere to bury the thing you're actually trying to say.

**The pre-conversation prep is often more valuable than the message.** Three specific sentences is often more useful than the message itself.

**This is not a substitute for judgment.** Claude doesn't know your rep. Use what it gives you as a starting point, then edit until it sounds like something you'd actually say to that specific person.
