# Skill 05 — The Capacity Model Framer

> *"They already said no. Coming back stronger doesn't mean coming back louder. It means coming back with a different argument."*

---

## What this is for

You asked for headcount. Someone up the chain said no, not yet, or come back with more. Now you have to figure out what "more" actually means — and make a case that changes the answer without burning the relationship or looking like you're just pushing harder on the same door.

The capacity conversation is hard for a specific reason: most managers ask for what they want instead of building to what the business needs. Finance and senior leadership don't think in headcount — they think in coverage ratios, pipeline risk, and revenue at stake. The translation between those two languages is where most capacity cases fall apart.

This prompt helps you build the scenario-based case — base, upside, downside — that gives a decision-maker something to act on instead of something to push back on.

---

## The moment this solves

You already got a no. Or you can see the no coming and want to get ahead of it. Either way, you need to walk into the next conversation with a model that shows what happens at current headcount, what becomes possible with one more hire, and what the cost of inaction actually is in pipeline terms.

Not a pitch. A business case with numbers they can stress-test.

---

## The base prompt

```
I need to build a capacity model to make the case for additional headcount 
on my BDR team. I've already been told no once and I need to come back 
with a stronger argument.

Here is my context:

**Current team:**
[How many BDRs, tenure breakdown, current attainment vs. target.]

**Current targets:**
[What is the team expected to produce?]

**What's actually happening:**
[Where are you falling short and why? Be specific and honest.]

**What I've already been told:**
[What was the objection when headcount was denied?]

**What I'm asking for:**
[Be honest about what you think you need.]

**The audience:**
[Who is making this decision and what do they care about?]

---

Give me:

1. **The reframe** — One paragraph that shifts the conversation from 
   "headcount request" to "pipeline risk." Name a number.

2. **The three scenarios:**

   Current state (no hire):
   - Projected monthly meetings at current capacity
   - Projected quarterly pipeline contribution
   - Gap vs. target in dollars

   Scenario A — One experienced hire:
   - Ramp timeline
   - Meeting contribution at Month 3, 6, 12
   - Pipeline impact at each stage

   Scenario B — One junior hire + enablement investment:
   - Same projections at lower cost
   - Where this beats Scenario A and where it falls short

3. **The ask, sized correctly** — What does the model support? 
   Not what I want — what the numbers point to.

4. **The objection I'll face** — Most likely pushback given my audience, 
   with a one-paragraph handle using model language.

5. **The one number** — For a 60-second hallway conversation.
```

---

## The "come back stronger" prompt

```
I was denied headcount and need a different argument, not a louder version 
of the same one.

**Original ask:** [What you asked for]
**The objection:** [What you were told]
**When this was:** [How long ago]
**What has changed:** [Anything different now, even small things]

1. Diagnose the original ask — why didn't it land?
2. Build the counter-case — a genuinely different argument that leads 
   with what the business loses by waiting. Supported by two numbers 
   I can defend under pushback.
```

---

## The spreadsheet builder

```
Build me a capacity model I can put in Google Sheets:

ASSUMPTIONS TAB: current headcount, ramp timeline, meetings per rep, 
pipeline value per meeting, salary range, manager time cost during ramp.

SCENARIOS TAB: Three columns (Current / Add Experienced / Add Junior)
Rows: meetings per month at Month 1/3/6/12, pipeline per quarter, 
gap vs. target in dollars, time to productivity, Year 1 cost, 
pipeline per dollar of hire cost.

SUMMARY TAB: headline number, recommendation, payback period per scenario.

Give me structure and formulas in plain language. Flag the 2-3 assumptions 
that will get challenged and tell me how to defend them.
```

---

## Make it yours

```
Build me a capacity context block grounded in my team's actual data. 
Ask me:

1. What does a fully ramped BDR produce per month — real number, not target?
2. How long does ramp actually take before a hire becomes productive?
3. What's the pipeline value per meeting in your market?
4. Who makes the final headcount call and what do they respond to most?
5. What objection or assumption consistently kills your capacity case?

Then write me a capacity context block.
```

---

## Tips from the field

**The reframe is the whole game.** Finance said no to a cost. Your job is to turn that cost into a comparison — not "what does this hire cost" but "what does not hiring cost."

**Let the model set the ask.** Ask for too much and you look unrealistic. Too little and you don't solve the problem. The scenario model tells you what the numbers support.

**The ramp objection is coming.** Build the fully-loaded cost at Month 3, 6, and 12 explicitly. Show that the pipeline gap during ramp costs more than the ramp itself.

**Know your one number.** Write it on a sticky note before any capacity conversation.

**Coming back after a no is a relationship move.** Acknowledge the original objection before launching the new model. "You told me to find efficiencies first. Here's what I found" beats launching straight into the new model.
