# Skill 08 — The Enablement Doc

> *"The play worked. Nobody wrote down why. Two weeks later half the team is running a version of it that doesn't work and nobody knows what changed."*

---

## What this is for

Something worked. A play converted. A rep cracked an objection nobody else had handled. A campaign generated pipeline at a rate the team hadn't seen before. And then the moment passed, the team moved on, and whatever made it work lived in one person's head instead of the whole team's hands.

The enablement doc captures the thing that worked before it disappears. Not a policy document, not a training manual — a short, opinionated brief that tells the rest of the team exactly what to replicate and why it worked.

The hardest part isn't writing it. It's knowing what to include and what to leave out. This prompt makes that decision for you.

---

## The moment this solves

You're in standup on a Friday. One of your reps just had their best week of the quarter. You ask them what was different and they give you a good answer. You think "the whole team needs to hear this" and then the meeting ends and by Monday it's gone.

This prompt turns that Friday moment into something the whole team is running by Tuesday.

---

## The base prompt

```
Build an enablement doc for my BDR team based on something that worked.

What worked:
[Describe the play, campaign, call approach, or objection handle. 
Be specific — include conversion rates if you have them.]

Why I think it worked:
[Your hypothesis. Be honest.]

What I want the team to replicate:
[The specific behavior or approach — not the exact words, the method.]

What I don't want them to copy blindly:
[Anything specific to this rep or situation that won't transfer.]

Who will use this:
[Team size and experience level.]

How I'll share it:
Async in Slack first, then walk through it live in standup.

---

First — before you write anything: tell me what to include and what to 
leave out. Give me 3-5 bullets of what belongs vs. what makes it longer 
without making it better.

Then build the doc:

WHAT THIS IS: [One sentence.]

WHY IT WORKED: [2-3 sentences. The honest analysis.]

HOW TO RUN IT: [Step by step. Short steps. Each one is an action.]

THE PART THAT'S EASY TO GET WRONG: [One paragraph. The surface-level 
copy that won't work.]

WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE: [One concrete example. Real subject line, real 
opening sentence. This is what reps open the doc for.]

THE NUMBERS: [1-2 data points. A benchmark to measure against.]

STANDUP TALKING POINTS: [3 bullets. 90 seconds to deliver. Creates 
anticipation before the doc goes out.]

Under 400 words. Readable in 3 minutes. Actionable in the next call block.
```

---

## The objection handle variant

When what worked was an objection handle:

```
One of my reps cracked an objection that's been killing our team.

**The objection:** [Exact wording prospects use]
**What used to happen:** [How reps handled it before]
**What this rep did differently:** [As specifically as possible]
**Why it worked:** [Your analysis]

Build a one-page objection handle doc:

THE OBJECTION (exact words)
WHY IT KILLS CALLS (what's really happening)
THE OLD HANDLE (what wasn't working and why)
THE NEW HANDLE (word for word)
THE PRINCIPLE UNDERNEATH (one sentence — the insight that transfers)
WHEN NOT TO USE IT (where this backfires)

Then give me a 60-second standup intro.
```

---

## The "what to include" prompt

When you're not sure what belongs in the doc, run this first:

```
I want to document something that worked but I'm not sure what to include.

Here is what happened: [Describe it]

Ask me three clarifying questions that will help determine:
1. What's replicable vs. specific to this situation or rep?
2. What's the minimum a rep needs to try this?
3. What looks like the method but will produce worse results if copied 
   without understanding?

After I answer, give me the include/exclude list before building anything.
```

---

## Make it yours

```
Build me an enablement doc template for how my team actually learns. Ask me:

1. When you share something async, what format do they actually engage with?
2. What's the average attention span for a doc you drop in Slack?
3. What makes your team take a doc seriously vs. file it away?
4. When you walk through something live in standup, what makes it land?
5. What's a piece of enablement you created that your team actually used — 
   what made it different?

Then write me a template and standup intro format that fits how my team works.
```

---

## Tips from the field

**The curation decision is more valuable than the writing.** Before you build anything, run the include/exclude step. Ask: "If a rep reads only one section, which one should it be?" That section is the doc.

**"What good looks like" is the section reps actually use.** A real subject line, a real opening sentence — this is what someone opens the doc for before a call block. Spend the most time here.

**The standup talking points change how the async lands.** Drop the doc with context, not silence. The talking points create anticipation before it goes out.

**Document while it's warm.** A rough doc on Monday is worth more than a polished doc next month that reconstructs something that has already faded.

**The principle underneath is what scales.** Reps who memorize the words stop when the script goes off-track. Reps who understand the principle adapt. Every doc needs one sentence that names it.
